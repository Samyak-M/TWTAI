## SLIDE 4 — The Library Analogy

# How LLMs Work
## The Library Analogy

Imagine reading **every book, article, and documentation set ever published online.**

Someone starts a sentence.
You complete it — based on everything you've read.

**That's roughly what an LLM does.**

- The "library" = a significant portion of the public internet
- The "reading" = training (happened before you use it)
- The "completion" = generated word by word, every time

## SLIDE 5 — Key Vocabulary

# Key Vocabulary

| Term | Plain-Language Meaning |
|------|----------------------|
| **LLM** | AI brain trained on text |
| **Token** | Roughly one word or word piece |
| **Context window** | What the model can "see" right now |
| **Prompt** | What you type to the AI |
| **Hallucination** | Plausible-sounding but wrong output |

> You don't need the math. You need the behavior.

## SLIDE 6 — What AI Is Good At

# What AI Does Well

- ✅ Recognizing patterns ("does this doc follow the structure?")
- ✅ Generating text in a given style
- ✅ Checking completeness against a checklist
- ✅ Reformatting and summarizing
- ✅ Applying style rules consistently

**AI is excellent at the *form* of documentation.**

---

## SLIDE 7 — What AI Needs You For

# What AI Needs YOU For

| Gap | Why You're Essential |
|-----|---------------------|
| **Factual accuracy** | AI doesn't know your product |
| **Audience awareness** | Will a junior dev understand this? |
| **Strategic decisions** | Should we document this at all? |
| **Missing context** | Why was this API deprecated? |
| **"Good enough" calls** | Is this ready to publish? |

**AI is great at form. You own the substance.**
